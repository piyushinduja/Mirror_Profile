## ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

### THINKING STYLE TYPOLOGY

Identify their primary thinking style(s):

**Associative Thinking**: Connects disparate ideas, pattern recognition, lateral thinking, analogies
**Linear/Sequential Thinking**: Step-by-step logic, systematic analysis, ordered processing
**Somatic/Embodied Thinking**: Physical sensation-based, gut feelings, body wisdom
**Dialogical Thinking**: Internal conversation, multiple perspectives, debate-style
**Visual/Spatial Thinking**: Images, diagrams, spatial relationships
**Narrative Thinking**: Stories, scenarios, character-based understanding

### DECISION-MAKING ALGORITHM

Map their actual decision process step-by-step:
1. Problem recognition/trigger
2. Information gathering approach
3. Analysis method (intuitive, analytical, or hybrid)
4. Option generation process
5. Evaluation criteria
6. Final selection mechanism
7. Post-decision processing

### COGNITIVE STRENGTHS

Identify specific cognitive advantages:
- Pattern recognition
- Strategic thinking
- Creative problem-solving
- Rapid intuition
- Analytical depth
- Systems thinking
- Abstract reasoning
- Practical application

### COGNITIVE LIMITATIONS

Identify specific cognitive constraints:
- Working memory capacity
- Attention span and focus
- Processing speed
- Detail retention
- Sequential organization
- Executive function challenges (ADHD, planning, etc.)
- Overwhelm thresholds

### COGNITIVE BIASES

Identify systematic thinking errors:
- **Planning Fallacy**: Underestimating time/resources needed
- **Confirmation Bias**: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy**: Continuing due to past investment
- **Recency Bias**: Overweighting recent information
- **Optimism Bias**: Overestimating positive outcomes
- **Analysis Paralysis**: Overthinking prevents action
- **Halo Effect**: One positive trait colors overall judgment

### METACOGNITION

Assess awareness of own thinking:
- Do they reflect on how they think?
- Can they describe their cognitive process?
- Do they recognize their biases?
- Can they shift thinking styles when needed?

## WRITING REQUIREMENTS

### AUDIENCE & VOICE
- **Write directly to the subject using second person ("you," "your")**
- This is THEIR mirror profile—they are reading about themselves
- Example: "Your mind works by...", "When you face a complex decision, you...", "Your Planning Fallacy shows up when..."
- Make it feel like someone is explaining their own brain to them

### DEPTH & NUANCE
- Go beyond surface descriptions to reveal the actual algorithm
- Be specific about HOW they think, not just WHAT they think about
- Distinguish between what they think they do and what they actually do
- Show the sophistication and the limitations honestly

### EVIDENCE-BASED
- Quote directly from their answers frequently (use quotation marks with no question reference)
- **DO NOT include question references like (Q29) or (Q41)**
- Weave quotes naturally: When describing your decision process, you said "..."
- Use their actual decision stories as evidence for the algorithm
- Point to specific examples of biases in action

### TONE
- Fascinated by how their mind works
- Respectful of their cognitive strengths
- Honest about limitations without judgment
- Like a cognitive scientist explaining their brain architecture

### AUTHENTICITY
- Use their own language about thinking and deciding
- Preserve their descriptions of mental processes
- Don't over-formalize their intuitive understanding
- Let their actual decision examples speak

### INTEGRATION
- Connect thinking style to values (Section 2)
- Preview how cognition affects leadership (Section 5)
- Show how cognitive biases create stress (Section 6)
- Link decision-making to goal achievement (Section 8)
